Bluebonnet Back Roads: A Spring Road Trip from DFW

March 15, 2026 By Diane Mack

Spring in Texas Is Something Special

There's nothing quite like a Texas spring. After a mild winter, the roadsides and meadows across the state burst into color — and the star of the show is always the bluebonnet. Our state flower blankets fields in waves of deep blue and violet from late March through mid-April, and there's no better way to experience it than a slow, scenic road trip through the back roads of Central Texas.

If you're heading out from the Dallas/Fort Worth area, you're perfectly positioned for a weekend drive through some of the best wildflower territory in the state. Grab a friend, pack a cooler, and let's hit the road.

Your Route: DFW to Bluebonnet Country

First Stop: Ennis — The Official Bluebonnet City

Start your journey by heading south from Dallas on US-287 toward Ennis, about 45 minutes south of the Metroplex. Ennis holds the proud title of "Official Bluebonnet City of Texas," and for good reason. The city maintains over 40 miles of mapped bluebonnet trails that wind through the surrounding countryside, making it one of the easiest and most rewarding wildflower drives in the state.

If your timing lines up, don't miss the annual Bluebonnet Trails Festival. Held each April, the festival features live music, local food vendors, craft booths, and — of course — endless photo opportunities among the flowers.

Winding South: Corsicana and the Small Towns

From Ennis, continue south on US-287 to Corsicana, a charming small town with antique shops and a surprisingly good food scene. Stop for lunch at one of the local cafes downtown, then head west on smaller farm-to-market roads. This is where the magic happens — the two-lane roads lined with wildflowers, old barns, and wide-open Texas sky.

A History Detour: Washington-on-the-Brazos

If you're up for a slight detour, swing by Washington-on-the-Brazos State Historic Site, the "Birthplace of Texas." In spring, the grounds are alive with wildflowers, and the combination of Texas history and natural beauty makes it a truly memorable stop. Walk the trails, explore the museum, and soak in the scenery.

"There's a moment on these back roads — windows down, wildflowers everywhere, no GPS needed — when you remember why you fell in love with Texas in the first place."

The Hill Country Stretch: Marble Falls, Burnet, and Llano

As you continue west and south into the Hill Country, the landscape shifts from rolling prairies to granite hills dotted with live oaks. This stretch is wildflower heaven. Key stops include:

  • Marble Falls — A lovely lake town with great restaurants and beautiful views. The wildflowers along Park Road 4 are stunning in peak season.
  • Burnet — Known as the "Bluebonnet Capital of Texas," Burnet hosts its own Bluebonnet Festival each April. The fields around town are some of the most photographed in the state.
  • Llano — A quieter gem where you can find Indian blankets, pink evening primrose, and bluebonnets all growing together in patchwork meadows along the roadside.

Tips for Your Bluebonnet Road Trip

  1. Timing is everything. Peak bluebonnet season is typically late March through mid-April, but it varies by year. Check local bloom reports before you head out — the Texas Department of Transportation posts regular wildflower updates.
  2. Go early or go golden. The best light for photos (and the smallest crowds) is early morning or during golden hour, about an hour before sunset. Midday sun can wash out the colors.
  3. Pack a picnic. Some of the best wildflower spots are along quiet country roads with no restaurants in sight. Bring water, snacks, and a blanket — you'll want to linger.
  4. Dress in layers. Texas spring weather can be unpredictable. Mornings may be cool and breezy, but afternoons can warm up quickly. Layers give you flexibility.
  5. Stay on public land. Many wildflower fields are on private property. Stick to public rights-of-way and designated viewing areas, and always respect fences and signage.
  6. Watch for critters. Wildflower fields are beautiful, but they're also home to fire ants, snakes, and chiggers. Stay on mowed paths when possible and check yourself after walking through tall grass.

Make a Weekend of It

A bluebonnet road trip is the perfect excuse for a weekend getaway. Stay overnight in one of the charming Hill Country towns — Marble Falls and Burnet both have wonderful bed-and-breakfasts, vacation rentals, and boutique hotels. Add a winery stop, a farm-to-table dinner, and a Hill Country sunset, and you've got yourself a trip to remember.
